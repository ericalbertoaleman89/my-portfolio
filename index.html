My Approach

"For me creating UX designs begins with a thorough understanding of the user. I start by defining the user persona and empathizing with their needs, ensuring that I grasp the challenges they face and the goals they aim to achieve. This initial step allows me to focus on crafting a solution that truly addresses their requirements. Following this, I move into the feature ideation phase, where I brainstorm and list out possible features that could meet the user's needs and solve their problems. This process helps me refine the concept and define a clear direction for the design, ensuring that every feature serves a purpose and aligns with the user's goals."

project crux

case study topic

Organization and resource management are the most important parts of any project. This goes doubly in the tech industry, which is riddled with complex folder structures and time constraints.

user profile

Who are we designing for?

Name: Alex
Age: 32
Gender: Male
Occupation: Tech Industry Designer
Income: $60,000 per year

Background:
Alex is a dedicated professional who spends most of his time creating content for games and software. Despite his clear goals and organizational skills, he struggles with project organization, often resulting in a maze of folders and disjointed notes. This challenge arises from the creative nature of his work, where many notes require specific contexts to make sense.

Technological Proficiency:
Alex is tech-savvy and proficient with smartphones. He prefers streamlined and user-friendly apps that save him time, valuing efficiency and seamless experiences.

Goals and Motivations:
Alex's main goal is to efficiently manage his projects and improve his organizational system to increase productivity and reduce stress.

Pain Points:
Organization is a significant pain point for Alex. He seeks solutions that simplify the organization of his projects and notes, making it easier to find and manage information.

Expectations:
Alex expects tools and software to have user-friendly interfaces that guide him effortlessly through the organization process. He values simplicity, clarity, and efficiency in the tools he uses.

Value proposition

process of identifying value proposition

After compiling a list of app feature ideas, we prioritize those that align with our users' problem statement. This ensures that our value proposition addresses their specific needs, recognizing that other users may have different requirements.

Value proposition:
Our app enables the effortless creation of labeled containers for uploading various types of content, including files, text information, and URLs. These containers can be arranged in a 2D space, allowing users to create clusters of related content for improved project management.

the challenge

unifying orginazation methods

Information management is a fractured process, but its components are all interconnected. For example, rebooting a PC means also taking time again to lay out windows, dig for files and reinitiate programs. Staying on track with a project is a time-consuming process in and of itself. My aim with this software is to employ human spatial awareness in streamlining tasks such as viewport layout, sorting, scheduling and goal setting for projects.

the human factor

Considerations for a more equitable design

Humans tend to have an easier time with spatial organization because it aligns with our natural way of organizing physical objects in the world. Spatial organization allows us to visually see and arrange items based on their relationships to each other, which can make it easier to remember where things are located. On the other hand, digital folders and data structures require us to categorize and label items based on more abstract concepts, which can be less intuitive for some people. However, digital organization offers advantages such as the ability to quickly search for and access information, as well as the ability to easily share and collaborate on data. Understanding these cognitive differences is crucial when designing project management and content organizational tools. By aligning with users' natural organizational tendencies, such tools can enhance user experience and productivity, ultimately creating a more intuitive and efficient workspace.

Competative audit

After ideation and developing several value propositions, we proceed to review other projects with similar goals and conduct a competitive audit. These audits are invaluable, offering insights and evidence while conserving resources. Some key questions we aim to address include:

How did our competitors approach the design of their products?

Is the competitor's solution difficult to use?

Are there user needs that your competitors do not meet?"

Clear app

The Clear app makes your paper “to do” list obsolete with its nifty alarm feature. It is also easy to use and look at. However, it is not designed to help with project scope or resource management.

axosoft

Axosoft’s app is awesome because it facilitates scrum. However, it does not provide the ability to organize and store files, and it is not as user friendly as the Clear app due to list-style menus that crowd the interface.

Design process

the product life cycle

The product life cycle is a process with many moving parts that can extend well beyond the product launch. The visual design process typically follows these steps: feature ideation, user flows, sketches, wireframes, redline specs, prototype, initial user tests, revisions, high-fidelity prototype, user tests, final aesthetic, continued data collection, and revisions.

the design

Why it looks the way it does.

My design is focused on browsing, labeling and placing large amounts of content, so I created an infinite space that the user can pan and zoom within. I used a skeuomorphic design for the header to make a clear distinction between the content in the viewport and the main app controls.

Much like the Axosoft design, this app uses the concept of scrum. However, the UI has been simplified by replacing words with recognizable icons, removing the need to clutter the screen with verbal menus. I included a legend for the icons that can be accessed via the question mark located on the bottom left of the viewport.

I took a cue from the Clear app and used Apple products as a reference, as their aesthetics are among the most popular in the tech world. My final design is reminiscent of iTunes: familiar and polished.

As a main interface color, I chose purple to evoke creativity, as this app is geared toward the production artists of the industry.
